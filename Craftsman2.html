The Master Craft of Uzbek Copper Engraving

Welcome to Misgarlik — The Art of Uzbek Coppersmithing

Uzbekistan is world-renowned for its applied arts, including embroidery, ganch carving, and wood carving. Among these, Misgarlik (Coppersmithing) is one of the most ancient and widely spread traditions. From the bustling markets of Bukhara to the workshops of Kokand, masters have transformed simple copper into exquisite jugs (oftoba), trays (lagan), and tea urns (samovar).

What is Misgarlik?

Misgarlik is the traditional craft of creating tools, household items, and decorative objects from copper. This ancient handicraft has been passed down through generations and remains a vital part of Central Asian cultural heritage. Master artisans use both casting and hammer-forging techniques to create functional vessels and artistic pieces that combine beauty with practicality.

Historical Significance

Copper has been known to humanity since ancient times, originally mined from Cyprus (hence its Latin name "cuprum"). Historical sources document that Uzbek coppersmithing flourished as early as the 10th century. The Arab geographer and traveler Al-Muqaddasi recorded that by the 10th century, large copper cauldrons, beautifully decorated vessels, and ornate oil lamps were being exported from Samarkand and Bukhara throughout the Arab world.

By the 11th and 12th centuries, Termez became famous throughout the world for its copperwork. The 12th and 13th centuries saw unprecedented attention to copper craftsmanship throughout Central Asia. During the 18th and 19th centuries, the cities of Bukhara, Samarkand, Kokand, and Khiva became renowned centers for producing high-quality bronze and copper vessels.

The Material: Why Copper?

Copper is a soft, ductile, and malleable metal with a reddish color. It conducts heat and electricity exceptionally well (second only to silver) and makes an ideal material for functional vessels. Historically, Uzbek craftsmen worked with pure copper using cold-forging techniques, creating soft yet durable items. Later, they developed bronze—an alloy of copper and tin—which produced even more durable and refined pieces.

Regional Schools and Techniques

Over centuries, distinctive coppersmithing schools developed in major cities: Kokand, Tashkent, Samarkand, Khiva, and others. Each school refined its own unique techniques and aesthetic traditions. Craftsmen use two primary methods: casting (melting and pouring copper) and hammer-forging (shaping cold copper with repeated hammering). Vessels are typically created in sections, then joined together. After initial construction, specialized engravers (kandakors) add intricate decorative patterns.


This website preserves the history, techniques, and distinct regional styles of Uzbekistan's copper engraving heritage.

Naqsh Turlari: The Language of Uzbek Copper Engraving

The Artistic Heritage of Uzbekistan is fundamentally defined by the intricate system of ornamentation known as naqsh. This sophisticated visual language encodes historical narratives, spiritual aspirations, and mathematical precision. For the scholar of Central Asian applied arts, naqsh is not merely decorative; it is a discipline serving as both a marker of socio-economic status and a vessel for cultural continuity over millennia.

The Taxonomy of Naqsh: Primary Patterns and Motifs

The decorative surface of an Uzbek copper vessel is a hierarchical arrangement of motifs designed to guide the eye across the object's form. The primary categories include vegetative (islimi), geometric (girih), zoomorphic, and architectural patterns.

The Philosophy and Application of Islimi

Islimi represents the most significant category of Uzbek ornamentation. Consisting of biomorphic, floral designs built upon the concept of the "eternal spiral," islimi sprouts leaves, buds, and flowers along an unbroken, flowing line. This motif represents the underlying order and unity of nature, reflecting a spiritual progression from singular source toward infinity.

Islimi Subdivisions:

  • Nim Islimi: Simplified, one-sided vegetative pattern used as borders
  • Pechak Islimi: Complex "winding" vine growing between parallel lines
  • Islimi-band: Complex interlacing of multiple islimi vines
  • Madohil Islimi: Interlocking elements for central medallions

Geometric Girih as Structural Framework

While islimi provides the "flesh" of ornamentation, girih (geometric pattern) serves as the "skeleton". Girih consists of intricate networks of stars, polygons, and interlocking lines representing mathematical perfection. In kandakorlik, girih is frequently used to organize space into discrete segments, within which organic islimi motifs are placed, representing the balance between absolute laws of the universe and fluid vitality of life.

The Bestiary of Metal: Zoomorphic Stylization

Despite the general Islamic preference for abstraction, Uzbek metalworkers maintain a rich vocabulary of stylized zoomorphic motifs, many carrying deep protective or symbolic meanings.

Key Zoomorphic Motifs:

  • Chashmi Bulbul: "Nightingale's Eye" - series of small, punched dots associated with wisdom and beauty
  • Ko'chkorak: "Ram's Horn" - ancient symbol of protection and power against evil
  • Pushti Baliq: "Fish Scales" - overlapping pattern symbolizing fertility and life-giving water
  • Ilon Izi: "Snake's Trail" - wavy border believed to protect contents from contamination
  • Kirmak: "Caterpillar/Leech" - winding pattern suggesting continuity and transformation

Compositional Archetypes in Copper Engraving

The organization of naqsh on a metal surface follows specific traditional compositions that determine the visual flow and hierarchy of decoration:

  • Gardish (Circular): Concentric rings radiating from central medallion (turunj)
  • Sitora (Star): Entire surface dominated by central star-like geometry with radiating rays
  • Mehrob (Arch): Series of interlocking arches mimicking mosque prayer niches
  • Mishkalak: Rectangular or square frames, similar to lattice structure
  • Gurunj: Oval or egg-shaped medallions filled with dense, "curly" islimi work

Semantics and Symbolism: The Protective Power of Naqsh

In the traditional Uzbek worldview, beauty was inseparable from spiritual and functional efficacy. Naqsh served as a form of "visual prayer" or protective talisman ensuring health and prosperity of the household.

Apotropaic Motifs (Evil Eye Protection):

  • Pepper (Qalampir): "Poisonous heat" believed to burn away evil intent
  • Knife (Pichoq): Sharp weapon cutting through misfortune
  • Lamp (Chiroq): Light of knowledge and divine protection
  • Almond (Bodom): Paradoxical symbol - sweet yet poisonous

Fertility and Abundance Motifs:

  • Pomegranate (Anorgul): Universal symbol of fertility and family prosperity
  • Willow (Majnuntol): Sacred tree driving away evil spirits
  • Fish (Baliq): Purity of water and reproductive health

Technical Methodologies: The Engraver's Toolkit

The creation of engraved copper is a laborious process requiring high degree of physical strength and fine motor control. The process is divided among three specialists: the misgar (coppersmith), rikhtagar (metal caster), and nakkoshzarbi (engraver).

Primary Engraving Techniques:

  • Kandakori (Deep Engraving): Carving deep into metal creating high-relief; background is textured with fine punch
  • Chizma (Shallow Engraving): Scratching or lightly chasing pattern without removing significant metal, allowing finer detail
  • Shabaka (Openwork): Metal completely cut through creating lace-like effect
  • Mor-pech (Inlaying): Fine silver wires or semi-precious stones set into copper

Traditional Tools: Qalam (chisel), Bolgha (hammer), Pargor (compass), Randa (polishing tool)

The Enduring Significance of Naqsh

The analysis of Uzbek national patterns in copper engraving reveals a discipline far more than decorative craft—it is a profound synthesis of history, mathematics, and spiritual belief. The persistence of motifs like the islimi spiral, mehrob arch, and chashmi bulbul dot across centuries demonstrates the deep resilience of the Uzbek aesthetic identity.

These patterns serve as a bridge between ancient Silk Road traditions and contemporary aspirations of a modernizing nation. Whether it is the deep, rigorous relief of a Bukhara tray or the microscopic lines of a Kokand jug, naqsh remains the "eternal line" that defines the cultural landscape of Uzbekistan. As long as the nakkoshzarbi continues to strike his chisel against red copper, the visual language of the ancestors will continue to speak to the future.

Regional Schools

Buxoro Maktabi

Famous for elegant designs, "Chekma" (dotted background), and "Minoqoriy" (enamel painting).

Samarqand Maktabi

Known for geometric patterns and "Chorbarg" (four-petal flower) motifs. Historic center of trade.

Farg'ona (Qo'qon) Maktabi

Known for deep engraving, precise lines, and animal figures (lions, fish).

Xorazm (Xiva) Maktabi

Distinctive use of red or black varnish backgrounds and "Aylanma Islimiy" spirals.

Toshkent Maktabi

Simpler patterns with plain backgrounds. Famous for "Piltalik" tea urns.

Qarshi Maktabi

Known for inlaying colored glass (turquoise) and using colored varnishes.
